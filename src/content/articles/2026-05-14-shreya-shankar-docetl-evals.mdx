## TL;DR

Shreya Shankar's DocETL work is a practical warning: long-context models do not magically solve messy document analysis. You still need to decompose tasks, rewrite vague operations into clearer steps, and evaluate each step against what humans actually want.

## What's New

DocETL frames document processing as a pipeline that an agent can rewrite and optimize. Instead of asking a model to "analyze these documents," the system can split work into extraction, classification, aggregation, and validation steps.

Her validator paper adds a second lesson: evaluators also need validation. People often define criteria only after seeing model outputs. That means evaluation is not a one-time rubric-writing exercise. It is iterative.

Together, the work gives a concrete pattern for AI data processing: structure the task, inspect intermediate outputs, and keep refining the checks as real failures appear.

## Why It Matters

Many organizations have piles of unstructured text: contracts, support tickets, policy documents, medical notes, public records, and research PDFs. AI can help, but only if the pipeline is auditable.

If the model extracts the wrong entity or groups documents under the wrong label, a polished final answer hides the mistake. Step-level evals make those mistakes visible.

<PracticalGuide timeToTry="25 min" prerequisites={["A folder of messy documents", "One extraction or classification task"]}>

1. Pick one document task, such as extracting names, dates, claims, obligations, or complaints.
2. Break it into steps: read, extract, normalize, classify, aggregate, and verify.
3. For each step, write one check a human can inspect. Example: "Did every extracted date appear in the source text?"
4. Run the pipeline on 10 documents and inspect intermediate outputs, not only the final summary.
5. When you find a failure, update the step or the check before scaling to more documents.

</PracticalGuide>
