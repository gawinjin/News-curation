## TL;DR

OpenAI's Q1 2026 consumer data says ChatGPT usage is no longer just an early-adopter story. Messages increased across age groups, users over 35 gained share, adoption broadened across countries, and work use became more repeatable.

## What's New

The report says users with typically feminine names now account for over half of inferred-gender consumer usage, after reaching approximate parity last year. It also says messages from users over 35 gained share, even though under-35 users still represented the largest share of messages.

Geographically, the fastest-rising countries by messages per capita ranking included countries across Latin America and the Caribbean, Asia-Pacific, and Africa. OpenAI frames this as broadening adoption, not just growth in the most established markets.

At work, the report says creating written and visual materials still led, but more specialized tasks grew. That suggests people are moving from trying ChatGPT once to finding repeatable use cases.

## Why It Matters

The adoption curve changes the training problem. If AI use is spreading across age groups, countries, and job types, teams cannot assume everyone needs the same "intro to prompts" workshop.

People need examples close to their real work: a nurse documenting notes, a teacher drafting rubrics, a manager summarizing decisions, a parent planning paperwork, or a small business owner answering customers. The practical question is no longer "should we use AI?" It is "which repeatable task is worth improving first?"

<PracticalGuide timeToTry="10 min" prerequisites={["A team, class, or household where AI use is uneven"]}>

1. Ask three people what they already use AI for, if anything. Do not start with a tool demo.
2. Group answers into repeatable tasks: writing, summarizing, planning, research, translation, or data cleanup.
3. Pick one task that happens every week and has low risk if the first draft is imperfect.
4. Write one shared prompt template with inputs, output format, and review rules.
5. Revisit it after a week. Keep the template only if it saved time without lowering quality.

</PracticalGuide>
