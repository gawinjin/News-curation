## TL;DR

Karpathy's LLM Wiki pattern is turning into real local tools: instead of asking an AI to search raw files every time, you let it build and maintain a linked Markdown wiki from your sources. The result is a private knowledge base you can browse, query, and improve over time.

## What's New

Karpathy's April gist describes a three-layer setup: raw sources, a generated wiki, and a schema file that tells the agent how to maintain it. The key change from normal RAG is persistence. The AI does not just retrieve chunks at question time; it updates summaries, entities, contradictions, and links as new sources arrive.

Nihar Shrotri's `llm-wiki` repo turns that pattern into a local-first project using Ollama, Qwen3, Obsidian-style Markdown, and local search. It is still a builder-oriented tool, but the direction is useful for anyone who has scattered PDFs, saved articles, meeting notes, or research folders.

The practical idea is not that AI should replace your judgment. It is that AI can handle the upkeep that usually kills personal knowledge bases: filing, cross-linking, updating summaries, and checking whether old notes conflict with new ones.

## Why It Matters

Most personal notes die because they are write-only. You save something, forget the filename, and then ask ChatGPT the same question later from scratch.

An LLM-maintained wiki flips that habit. Your job is to curate sources and ask better questions. The model's job is the boring maintenance: make pages, connect them, cite sources, and keep the index useful. Because it can run locally, it is also a better fit for sensitive personal or work documents than uploading everything to a cloud notebook.

<PracticalGuide timeToTry="20 min" prerequisites={["A folder of notes, PDFs, or saved articles", "Ollama or another local LLM setup"]}>

1. Create a small test folder with 5 to 10 documents on one topic. Do not start with your whole archive.
2. Read Karpathy's gist and copy the three-layer idea into a short `AGENTS.md` or `CLAUDE.md`: raw sources, generated wiki, and rules.
3. Install or inspect a local implementation such as `NiharShrotri/llm-wiki`, or ask your coding agent to build the simplest version: `raw/`, `wiki/`, `index.md`, and `log.md`.
4. Ingest one source at a time. After each run, open the generated pages in Obsidian or your editor and check whether the links and summaries are useful.
5. Ask one question you actually care about, then save the answer back into the wiki as a synthesis page if it is worth keeping.

</PracticalGuide>
