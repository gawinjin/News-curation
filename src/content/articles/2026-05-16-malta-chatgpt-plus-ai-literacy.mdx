## TL;DR

OpenAI and Malta announced a program that gives citizens access to ChatGPT Plus after completing an AI literacy course. The important idea is the pairing: access alone is not enough. People need a basic course, examples, and rules for responsible use.

## What's New

Malta's AI for All initiative will offer a University of Malta course on what AI can and cannot do, responsible use, and practical everyday applications. After completing the course, eligible citizens can receive one year of ChatGPT Plus at no cost to them.

The first phase starts in May, with Malta's Digital Innovation Authority managing distribution. OpenAI frames this as part of its OpenAI for Countries work with governments and institutions.

This is not just a product promotion. It is an adoption pattern: local curriculum, clear eligibility, tool access, and a national body responsible for rollout.

## Why It Matters

Many organizations are making the same mistake at smaller scale: they buy AI seats, then assume people will naturally use them well. Most people need examples close to their real life.

Malta's model suggests a better sequence. Teach basic judgment first, then provide access, then measure whether people use the tool for learning, work, public services, and everyday tasks.

<PracticalGuide timeToTry="25 min" prerequisites={["A team, school, or community group", "One AI tool people already ask about"]}>

1. Write a one-page "AI starter course" for your group: what the tool is good at, what it is bad at, and what people should never paste into it.
2. Pick three local examples, such as drafting emails, summarizing long documents, preparing study notes, or planning a small project.
3. Require people to complete the starter course before giving paid access or recommending daily use.
4. Add a feedback form with two questions: "What did it help with?" and "Where did it give a questionable answer?"
5. Review the answers after two weeks and update the examples before expanding access.
