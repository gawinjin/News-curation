## TL;DR

Nathan Lambert's open-model argument is that open ecosystems can compound when labs share enough reports, tools, and recipes to avoid repeating each other's R&D. The practical question for users is not "open or closed?" It is "where will learning accumulate fastest?"

## What's New

His recent Interconnects piece looks at China's open-first AI ecosystem and asks whether shared technical reports and open releases can reduce future development cost. The key distinction is between the final training run and the many experiments needed before that run.

If most cost sits in research, infrastructure, tuning, and failed attempts, then shared knowledge matters. Open models are not always cheaper to deploy out of the box, but open ecosystems can make future iteration cheaper for everyone who learns from the same artifacts.

The RLHF Book shows the same character in a different form: make the hidden craft of post-training more explicit so builders can reason from shared concepts instead of rumors.

## Why It Matters

For normal users and small teams, model choice is not just a benchmark table. Closed APIs may be cheaper and easier for simple use. Open models may be better when you need privacy, control, customization, or learning from the broader ecosystem.

Lambert's frame helps you ask a better question: will this choice help you learn and adapt, or only rent a black box?

<PracticalGuide timeToTry="15 min" prerequisites={["One AI tool or model choice you are evaluating"]}>

1. Write down your use case: chat, coding, document extraction, classification, local privacy, or fine-tuning.
2. Compare one closed model and one open model on the same 10 examples.
3. Add an ecosystem column: docs, community examples, evals, deployment tools, tuning recipes, and issue activity.
4. Decide whether your bottleneck is price, quality, privacy, latency, or learning speed.
5. Choose the model path that improves that bottleneck, not the one with the loudest launch.
